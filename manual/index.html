NPT Manual

Introduction

The Network Planning Tool for Scotland is a planning support system, research project, and web application to support strategic active travel network planning. The 2023 version is focused on cycle network planning. The Network Planning Tool builds on the Propensity to Cycle Tool for England and Wales, which is a publicly available web application providing evidence on levels of cycling and potential down to the road network nationwide (Lovelace et al. 2017).

Note: the NPT is currently a prototype. Version 1 is expected to be completed by the end of 2023. The prototype version is provided to preview the tool and gather feedback. While the current version’s results are comprehensive and objective, tool outputs may change without warning. The prototype version is based only on travel-to-work data, and the results should be interpreted accordingly.

The University of Leeds produces the NPT on behalf of Sustrans and Transport Scotland.

 

NPT Essentials

The NPT is Scotland’s nationwide web-based strategic network planning tool that estimates cycling potential down to the street level.

The NPT can be thought of as:

i) the map user interface, 

ii) a series of open access data downloads that can be analysed in GIS software or statistical software, 

iii) the code base that can be modified to fix bugs, add new features, and develop new scenarios.

The NPT is an open-source tool, meaning the source code is transparent and in the public domain for others to learn from and build on. The code underlying the project is available to be copied and improved by the community.

The online interface can serve many needs, including gaining insight into the network of routes where cycling potential is highest and quantifying the benefits of cycling uptake. However, we recommend that intermediate and advanced users download data on zones, routes and route networks for in-house analysis.

The NPT allows users to visualise results baseline data and how cycling would change under different scenarios. The journey purposes and scenarios are described in detail below.

The NPT is a Progressive Web App (PWA), which can be installed on many devices, including your smartphone. The App provides the same features as the website. Still, it includes additional benefits such as pining the App to your device’s home screen and full-screen support.

Map Layers

The NPT allows you to view a broad range of data about cycling. This section describes each layer and its features.

Route Network

The route network is the primary layer in the NPT and the only one that is on by default when the tool loads. The route network estimates the number of cyclists on each road. It is designed to emphasise roads with lots of cycling potential and so aid planners in deciding where cycling infrastructure is needed.

The route network provides a range of options and filters to allow you to view different networks based on different journey purposes and assumptions.

Trip purpose

People have many reasons to travel, and their reasons for travel often change their choice of destinations and routes. Therefore, a cycling network designed for commuters may look very different to a network designed for children to travel to school. The trip purpose drop-down allows you to view different networks based on different trip purposes.

All

This is the default view that displays all journey purposes combined, offering a comprehensive overview of the total cycling demand.

 

Commute

The commute network, as the second option in the travel purpose dropdown list, is based on the 2011 Census travel to work data. Commuters tend to favour radial routes from suburban residential areas into town and city centres where most jobs are concentrated.

Primary School

The primary school network, as the third option in the travel purpose dropdown list, is dedicated to capturing the cycling patterns of those travelling to and from primary schools. It provides insights into the routes chosen by children and their guardians. Recognising these patterns is pivotal for urban planners, enabling them to emphasize and develop infrastructure that prioritises the safety of young cyclists. This ensures that journeys to primary schools are both safe and efficient for our youngest members of the community.

Secondary School

The secondary school network, as the forth option in the travel purpose dropdown list, is dedicated to understanding the cycling habits of those commuting to and from secondary schools. It offers insights into the pathways preferred by teenagers and their guardians. By identifying these trends, urban planners can focus on enhancing infrastructure that ensures the safety and efficiency of adolescent cyclists, facilitating their journeys to and from school in a secure manner.

Social – Coming soon

The social network is dedicated to capturing the cycling patterns of individuals travelling to social hubs, parks, and recreational areas. It sheds light on the routes most frequented for leisure and social activities. Recognizing these patterns is essential for urban planners, allowing them to prioritize and develop infrastructure that enhances the cycling experience, ensuring that social outings on bikes are both enjoyable and safe.

Shopping – Coming soon

The shopping network is dedicated to highlighting the cycling behaviours of individuals travelling to shopping centres, markets, and retail districts. It provides insights into the most popular routes for shopping-related trips. With this knowledge, urban planners can emphasize the creation of infrastructure that caters to the needs of shoppers on bikes, ensuring convenient and safe access to retail areas.

Personal – Coming soon

The personal network is dedicated to capturing the cycling patterns of individuals undertaking personal errands, such as visiting banks, post offices, or attending medical appointments. It offers insights into the routes chosen for these personal tasks. Understanding these patterns is crucial for urban planners, enabling them to design and prioritize infrastructure that makes personal errands on bikes both efficient and secure.

What’s missing from the NPT

The NPT does not currently consider leisure or recreational cycling. This is more complex to model as cyclists may not have a specific destination in mind.

The NPT currently only considers direct journeys where the whole trip is by bicycle. It does not consider mixed-mode journeys such as cycling to the station and then taking a train to your final destination. This means that the NPT slightly underestimates cycling potential overall and may significantly underestimate cycling potential in specific places (such as around train stations).

Network type

The 'network type' reflects choices cyclists make about route choice. There is strong evidence that cyclists prefer the most direct route, and it reduces journey times and the physical effort of cycling.

The need to prioritise creation of a network of safe & direct cycle routes, often or arterial corridors, is central to Scottish Government cycling policy & guidance - including Cycling By Design - & reflected in Sustrans own Places For Everyone infrastructure fund criteria.

However, until such a safe & direct network is created, cyclists may make detours away from roads that are (or are perceived to be) dangerous. There is strong evidence that safety concerns are the main barrier to more people cycling.

Cyclestreets calculate the routes taken by cyclists, and each network type is based on one of their routeing algorithms. The route choices are based on the current road infrastructure and don’t account for planned improvements or missing links.

Examples of the four network types in Edinburgh show how different assumptions about the routes cyclists take affect where the busiest parts (pink) of the network are.

Note that the choice of network type does not just change the routes cyclists take but also the number of cyclists predicted under each scenario. 

Fast/Direct (preferred)

This network type should be treated as the default.

The fastest network is based on cyclists taking the most direct (legal) routes. While cyclists prefer direct routes, this will often bring them onto busy and dangerous major roads, which are a barrier to cycling. Planners seeking to maximise cycling will build high-quality cycle infrastructure along busy roads, which form part of the fastest cycle route network.

Sustrans anticipate that:

  • Most new cycle network proposals will be based on joining up the fast/direct routes with the highest numbers of cyclists to create a genuinely dense & coherent network.
  • Supplementary guidance on how the tool should be used to generate these dense & coherent cycle networks will be issued by Sustrans during the course of 2023 (as the tool develops).
  • The majority of new individual cycle infrastructure proposals will have high number of cyclists (relative to the appropriate local context).
Quiet/indirect

The quiet network assumes that cyclists will avoid busy roads and be willing to take significant detours. While directing cyclists away from busy roads and onto quieter back streets may seem like a good idea, it can have significant downsides. Quiet routes are always longer and often much more challenging to navigate as they weave around the back streets. Even if the roads are safe, longer and more complex journeys discourage cycling. The NPT captures this effect, and the total number of cyclists on the quiet route network is less than on the fast route network.

As noted, Scottish Government policy & guidance & PFE fund criteria, now requires that most new cycling infrastructure is located on the most direct (often arterial) routes.

According, Sustrans anticipate that the ‘Quiet/Indirect’ network type will likely only rarely be used to:

  • Identify potential low cost/high impact ‘quick wins’ where meaningful additional to the cycle network can be made in the very short term eg by rapidly filtering residential streets parallel to arterial roads.
  • Supporting the design of Low Traffic or 20minute Neighbourhoods.

Quiet networks work best when the directness penalty is small. For example, a city with a grid layout could alternate between roads designed for cars and streets designed for active travel.

The image above (source) shows how Amsterdam uses its grid layout to have parallel but separate networks for cars (red, orange, black) and bicycles (green). Notice how the cycling network is much denser than the car network, ensuring that cyclists almost always benefit from a more direct route.

EBikes

The ebikes network is a variant of the fastest network that reduces the penalty for going up hills. Pedal cyclists incur a significant time and effort penalty from going uphill. Hence, a longer but flatter route is often faster. A good ebike can enable cyclists to ride uphill at 15 mph without breaking a sweat. Thus ebike riders may choose shorter but hillier routes than pedal cyclists.

As ebikes increase a cyclist’s range and carrying capacity while reducing effort and journey times, a world with many ebikes would expect higher levels of cycling than one with only pedal cycles.

 

Scenarios

The scenarios drop-down allows you to view different levels of cycling and other information about the road network

Baseline 

The baseline scenario represents the current level of cycling. As such, it is intended to show where there is an existing demand for cycling infrastructure.

Go Dutch

The Go Dutch scenario imagines a future where people in the UK are as likely to travel by bike as people in the Netherlands while accounting for differences in trip distance and hilliness between locations. As such, the network shows where there could be future demand for cycling infrastructure.

Planners should seek to design cycle networks that meet the needs of both current and future cyclists. But they may phase the construction to prioritise roads that will meet the needs of current cyclists and enable new people to cycle.

Line Colour

The line colour option allows you to visualise different characteristics of the route network. Below the line colour option is a contextual legend which shows the meaning of the colours on the map.

Number of cyclists

The number of cyclists is the default view. It shows an estimate of the number of cyclists on each road for the selected trip purpose, network type, and scenario. The thickness of the lines in the route network is also defined by the number of cyclists, with thicker lines representing more cyclists.

Cycle friendliness

Cycle friendliness is a subjective measure representing the quality of a route segment (a section of road or path) for cycling, with a score between 0 (very low quality) and 100 (very high quality). It considers a range of factors, using data derived from OpenStreetMap.

Factors that contribute to a higher score of cycle friendliness include (as appropriate):

  • Whether the cycleway is shared with motor vehicles or pedestrians,
  • The type of road
  • Presence of cycle infrastructure
  • Speed limit
  • Surface quality
  • Cycle signage, 
  • Any barriers or obstructions
  • Path width
  • Route legibility

See CycleStreets for further information, the term ‘quietness’ is used for the same measure that we call ‘cycle friendliness’.

 

Gradient

The average gradient of the road is shown as a percentage. Steeper roads are a barrier to cycling and affect route choice and the uptake of cycling in the scenarios.

Popup

Clicking on any road in the route network shows the route network popup.

The popup provides a summary table for all the information available about the route network. The table displays the number of cyclists under each scenario and network type. Below the table, the average gradient of the road and its cycle friendliness score are shown, along with a link to view the road in Google Street View (where available).

Route Network Filters

The sliders allow you to show/hide parts of the route network. You can filter on three variables:

 

Numbers of cyclists

This filter is only available when “Line colour” is set to a cycling scenario.

Gradient

Set the maximum and minimum gradient of roads that are visible. Gradient measures the average gradient of the road segment as a percentage. E.g. 0% = flat, 100% = vertical cliff.

Cycle friendliness

Set the maximum and minimum quietness of roads that are visible. Quietness measures how cycle friendly the existing road is, from hostile (least friendly) to quiet (most friendly).

Data Zones

Data Zones are small statistical neighbourhoods created for the Census. In the NPT, they are used to provide contextual area-based information.

The data zone options are:

  • Population density (per hectare)
  • Index of Multiple Deprivation (2020)
  • Drive time to a petrol station
  • Drive time to GP
  • Drive time to post office
  • Drive time to a retail centre
  • Drive time to primary school
  • Drive time to secondary school
  • Public transport time to GP
  • Public transport time to a post office
  • Public transport time to a retail centre
  • % without superfast broadband

Dasymetric mode

By default, the data zones are shown as a dasymetric map. This means that the buildings within a zone are coloured to display information. If you turn off dasymetric mode, a simple choropleth map is shown where the whole Data Zone is coloured. Note that the same underlying data is being visualised in both modes, and the differences between modes are merely aesthetic. However, dasymetric maps are intended to better represent the data by emphasising the true locations of people who are not uniformly distributed across the Data Zone.
